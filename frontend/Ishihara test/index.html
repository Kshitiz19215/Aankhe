Ishihara Test

The Ishihara test is a color perception test designed to assess a person's ability to differentiate between colors, particularly to identify red and green colors. It is named after its creator, Dr. Shinobu Ishihara, who developed the test in 1917.

The test consists of a series of plates, each containing a circle of dots appearing to be a random pattern of colors and sizes. Within these dots, there is a hidden numeral or shape that is easily visible to individuals with normal color vision but may be challenging or impossible for those with color vision deficiencies, such as red-green color blindness.

The Ishihara test is commonly used by optometrists and ophthalmologists as part of a comprehensive eye examination. It helps identify color vision deficiencies and determine the type and severity of color blindness in individuals. While the Ishihara test is widely used, it is not the only method for assessing color vision, and other tests may be employed for more detailed evaluation if needed.
